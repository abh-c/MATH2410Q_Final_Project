Riccati Equations

Riccatti equations take the form: \[y'=q_0(x)+q_1(x)y+q_2(x)y^2\] where \(q_0 \ne0\) (because that would be a Bernoulli equation) and where \(q_2\ne 0\) (because that would be a first order linear equation)1

These equations can be solved using two substitutions: \(t=q_2y\) and \(t=\frac{-\phi'}{\phi}\)2. The point of these substitutions is to obtain a linear equation that can be used to obtain the solution \(y\).

Example: solve \[y'=1+xy^2\] In this case, \(q_2=x\), so we can use the substitution \(t=xy\). We want to find the derivative of \(t\):

\[t'=y+xy'\]

Now we can substitute \(y'=1+xy^2\) and then \(y=t/x\) to get the equation entirely in terms of \(t\):

\[t'=y+x(1+xy^2)=y+x+x^2y^2\]

\[t'=\frac{t}{x}+x+x^2(\frac{t}{x})^2=x+\frac{t}{x}+t^2\]

Now we can use the substitution \(t=\frac{-\phi'}{\phi}\). First we can determine that \[t'=\frac{-\phi''}{\phi}+(\frac{\phi'}{\phi})^2\]

Since \(t^2=(\frac{-\phi'}{\phi})^2=(\frac{\phi'}{\phi})^2\), we can substitute that in. We can also substitute in \(t'=x+\frac{t}{x}+t^2\) and \(t=\frac{-\phi'}{\phi}\):

\[x+\frac{t}{x}+t^2=\frac{-\phi''}{\phi}+t^2\]

\[\frac{\phi''}{\phi}=-x+\frac{\phi'}{x\phi}\]

\[\phi''-\frac{1}{x}\phi'+x\phi=0\]

We have successfully reduced the problem down to a second order homogeneous equation. Because \(y=\frac{t}{x}=\frac{-\phi'}{x\phi}\), in order to get the solutions \(y_1\) and \(y_2\), all you have to do is solve for \(\phi_1\) and \(\phi_2\) and substitute to get a general equation of the form: \[y=c_1\frac{-\phi_1'}{x\phi_1}+c_2\frac{-\phi_2'}{x\phi_2}\]

You may be able to solve for \(\phi\) in terms of series or the Laplace transform.
