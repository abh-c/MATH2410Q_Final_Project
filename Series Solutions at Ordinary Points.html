Series Solutions at Ordinary Points

Consider an ODE of the form \(a_2(x)y'' + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = 0\), where \(a_2(x)\), \(a_1(x)\), and \(a_0(x)\) are polynomials that do not share any common factors. If \(a_2(x_0) \ne 0\) then \(x_0\) is an ordinary point.

Note: for a general ODE of the form \(y''+P(x)y'+G(x)y=0\), if \(P\) and \(G\) are both analytic at some point \(x=x_0\)—meaning there exists a power series centered at \(x_0\) that converges in some interval around \(x_0\)—then \(x_0\) is an ordinary point.1

The general method to solve such an ODE is to set \(y\) equal to a general series \[y = \sum^\infty_{n=0}c_n(x-x_0)^n\]then get \(y'\) and \(y''\) in terms of that series, substitute values into the ODE, then solve for the recurrence relation, then use that to find two solutions to the ODE \(y=c_0y_1+c_1y_2\), where \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are in terms of a series.

Generally, for an ordinary point for a second order, linear, homogeneous equation \(y''+P(x)y'+G(x)y=0\) where \(P\) and \(G\) are analytic at \(x_0\), there are guaranteed to exist 2 linearly independent solutions that can be expressed as series \(y = \sum^\infty_{n=0}c_n(x-x_0)^n\). These solutions have a radius of convergence within the distance from the ordinary point to the nearest singular points.1

Example: Solve \[y'' -y=0\]

Because \(a_2(x) = 1 \ne 0\) at every point, any point of \(x\) is an ordinary point. For simplicity choose \(x_0=0\) so that the series can be set around 0. Set \(y\) equal to a general series, then find its first and second derivatives:

\[y = \sum^\infty_{n=0}c_nx^n = c_0 + c_1x + c_2x^2 + \cdots\]

\[\to y' = \sum^\infty_{n=1}nc_nx^{n-1}\]

\[\to y'' = \sum^\infty_{n=2}n(n-1)c_nx^{n-2}\] Note: You could have the initial index \(n\) equal to \(0\) for both the first and second derivatives and have equivalent series. For \(y'\), the \(n\) multiple in the expression will cause the \(n=0\) term of the series to equal \(0\), and for \(y''\), the \(n(n-1)\) in the expression will cause the \(n=0\) and \(n=1\) terms to equal \(0\). However, it is usually useful to keep the indices \(1\) and \(2\) respectively when solving the problem.

Now substitute these series into the ODE:

\[\sum^\infty_{n=2}n(n-1)c_nx^{n-2}-\sum^\infty_{n=0}c_nx^n = 0\]

Now, to add the sums, it is advantageous to have the exponents of all the \(x\) terms the same so that the \(x^n\) term can be factored out of the expression.

We can reindex the sums by replacing the indices so that there is an \(x^k\) term in each series: - left sum: substitute \(k = n - 2\) - right sum: substitute \(k = n\)

\[\to \sum^\infty_{k=0}(k+2)(k+1)c_{k+2}x^{k}-\sum^\infty_{k=0}c_kx^k = 0\] \[=\sum_{k=0}^\infty((k+2)(k+1)c_{k+2}-c_k)x^k=0\]

Side Note: if, in a problem, series in the equation do not have the same initial index, rewrite a series by “taking out” as many terms as necessary to change the initial index. Generally, that process will look like: \[\sum_{k=0}^\infty(\text{some expresssion})x^k= (k=0\text{ term}) + \sum^\infty_{k=1}(\text{some expression})x^k = 0\]

For this expression, the series term is a sum of coefficient multiples of powers of \(x\) that looks something like \(C_1x^1+C_2x^2+C_3x^3+\cdots\). No matter what the coefficients are, because the \(x\) terms have different exponents, they will not cancel out. The only case in which the sum does not contain \(x\) terms is the case where all the coefficients equal 0 and where the entire sum also equals 0.

If all the coefficients equal 0, then the constant term in the sum can be set to equal 0 for whole number \(k\): \((k+2)(k+1)c_{k+2}-c_k=0\). This can be rearranged to obtain the equation: \[c_{k+2} = \frac{1}{(k+2)(k+1)}c_k\] This is true for \(k = 1, 2, 3, \cdots\). This equation is called a recurrence relation. It is used to find the relationship between sequential values of the constant \(c_n\) in the the solution \(y=\sum c_nx^n\).

With this information, a table can be made to solve for some values of \(c\) in terms of \(c_0\) and \(c_1\):

Constants in terms of \(c_0\):

  \(c_0\) arbitrary
\(k=0\) \(c_2\) \(\frac{1}{2}c_0\)
\(k=2\) \(c_4\) \(\frac{1}{4\cdot3}c_2 = \frac{1}{4\cdot3\cdot2}c_0\)
\(k=4\) \(c_6\) \(\frac{1}{6!}c_0\)

Constants in terms of \(c_1\):

  \(c_1\) arbitrary
\(k=1\) \(c_3\) \(\frac{1}{3\cdot2}c_1\)
\(k=3\) \(c_5\) \(\frac{1}{5\cdot4}c_3 = \frac{1}{5!}c_1\)
\(k=5\) \(c_7\) \(\frac{1}{7!}c_1\)

Only enough of these should be found to get the pattern.

Note: the specific values of \(c_0\) and \(c_1\) are arbitrary because for the solution \(y\), \(c_0 = y(0)\) and \(c_1 = y'(0)\) depending on the initial conditions. In the solution form, \(y=c_0y_1+c_1y_2\), where \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are 2 linearly independent functions.

Substitute these constants back into expansion:

\[y = c_0 + c_1x +\frac{1}{2}c_0x^2 + \frac{1}{3!}c_1x^3+\frac{1}{4!}c_0x^4+\frac{1}{5!}c_1x^5 + \cdots\] Now these terms can be grouped based on whether the constant depends on \(c_0\) or \(c_1\): \[y = c_0(1+\frac{1}{2}x^2+\frac{1}{4!}x^4 + \cdots) + c_1(x+\frac{1}{3!}x^3 + \frac{1}{5!}x^5+ \cdots)\] We’ve found the solutions \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) in the form \(y=c_0y_1+c_1y_2\).
