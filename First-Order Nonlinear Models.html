First-Order Nonlinear Models

Although most of the solution methods in this course have involved linear equations, many differential equations that model real-life phenomena are nonlinear. An example is the logistic growth model \[\frac{dP}{dt}=rP\left(1-\frac{P}{K}\right)\] where \(K\) is the carrying capacity and \(r\) indicates the growth rate

This model can produce an “S” shaped curve that initially looks exponential but then flattens out as it approaches the carrying capacity.

After expanding the equation out, we get: \[P'=rP-\frac{r}{K}P^2\] The \(P^2\) term indicates that this is a nonlinear ODE and an autonomous equation.

Example:

If we wanted to use this equation to model, for example, the growth of the population of bacteria in a petri dish, we could do so. Say we have an initial population \(P(0)=100\), a growth rate of \(r=0.25\) and a carrying capacity (based on the nutrients in the petri dish) of \(K=1,000,000\). We can solve the logistic growth model as a separable equation:

\[\frac{dP}{dt}=0.25P\left(1-\frac{P}{1,000,000}\right)\] \[\int\frac{1}{P(1-10^{-6}P)}\,dP=\int0.25\,dt\] The left integral can be obtained using partial fraction decomposition \[\int\frac{1}{P}+\frac{10^{-6}}{1-10^{-6}P}\,dP=\int0.25\,dt\] \[\ln P -\ln(1-10^{-6}P)=0.25t+C\] In this case, we’re assuming \(P\) is positive and that \(10^{-6}P\lt1\) to eliminate the absolute value signs in the natural logs.

\[\ln \frac{P}{1-10^{-6}P}=0.25t+C\]

\[\frac{P}{1-10^{-6}P}=Ce^{0.25t}\] \[P=\frac{Ce^{0.25t}}{1+C10^{-6}e^{0.25t}}\]

We can use initial conditions to find \(C\): \[\ln 100 -\ln(1-10^{-6}\times100)=0+C\quad\to C=4.61\]

The final growth model is: \[P=\frac{4.61e^{0.25t}}{1+4.61\times 10^{-6}e^{0.25t}}\]
