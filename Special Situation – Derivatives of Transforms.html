Derivatives of Transforms

Derivative of a transform with respect to \(s\): \(\frac{d}{ds}F(s)\) produces a nice pattern with its inverse \(f(t)\):\[(-1)^n\frac{d^n}{ds^n}[F(s)]=\mathscr{L}\{t^nf(t)\}\] This relation can be interpreted as the derivatives of \(F(s)\) with alternating sign correspond to \(f(t)\) functions multiplied by increasing degrees of \(t^n\).

This relation is useful in when you have the pattern \(t^nf(t)\) where obtaining the transform of a certain function would be a nightmare otherwise.

Example: obtain the Laplace transform of \[f(t)=t\sin(t)\] If we look at the right side of the equation \(t\sin(t)\) in terms of the derivative pattern \(t^nf(t)\), then \(n=1\) and \(f(t)=\sin(t)\). Therefore, the Laplace transform of \(t\sin(t)\) would equal: \[(-1)\frac{d}{ds}[\mathscr{L}\{\sin t\}]=-\frac{d}{ds}\left[\frac{1}{s^2+1}\right]\] \[=\frac{2s}{(s^2+1)^2}\] Using this pattern made finding the transform much easier.
