Solving a Second-Order IVP with the Laplace Transform

Using the Laplace transforms of derivatives of y, we can also solve second order IVPs in the same way as first order IVPs.

Example: Solve \[y''-y=x \qquad y(0)=0, y'(0)=0\] First, take the Laplace transform of moth sides:\[\mathscr{L}\{y''\}-\mathscr{L}\{y\}=\mathscr{L}\{t\}\] \[s^2Y(s)-sy(0)-y'(0)-Y(s)=\frac{1}{s^2}\] Now solve for \(Y(s)\) and substitute the initial conditions. In this case, the terms \(-sy(0)\) and \(-y'(0)\) will cancel out to zero.

\[Y(s) =\frac{1}{(s^2-1)(s^2)}\] At this point, partial fraction decomposition is required to split this fraction into two terms. The result of this should be: \[Y(s)=\frac{1}{s^2-1}+\frac{1}{s^2}\] Now, we can perform the inverse Laplace transform on both sides to obtain the solution: \[\mathscr{L}^{-1}\{Y(s)\}=\mathscr{L}^{-1}\left\{\frac{1}{s^2-1}\right\}+\mathscr{L}^{-1}\left\{\frac{1}{s^2}\right\}\] \[y(t)=\sinh t+t\] At this point, the IVP has been solved
